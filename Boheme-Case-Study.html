Boheme Thrift Store Website

Boheme Thrift Store Webpage

As the Davis Computer Science Club President I piloted a new program to help small businesses in Davis form effective UI/UX solutions in partnership with the Davis Downtown Business Association.

Opportunity!

This project was a fantastic opportunity to understand how to tailor a user experience toward meeting a company’s aesthetic goals and branding.

Process

Research

What is wrong with the current website?

To get started with the user research it was important to pain points that the current website had to ensure that our rehaul addressed and solved such concerns.

  • 1. Confusing User Journey

  • Boheme’s website compared to our other competitors’ website provides a more difficult user journey with a cluttered navigation bar. This leaves the user lost in the journey.

  • 2. Information Heavy

  • Boheme’s current website has too much content as compared to our competitors. Users would need to dig for information and the amount of clutter is overwhelming.

  • 3. Dated Visual Design

  • Boheme’s website is lacking the most on quality images/graphics that are outdated along with images that have failed to upload. With that, the visual design is outdated.

Competitive Analysis

After understanding what was wrong with the current website, I wanted to direct the team to compare it with other local businesses in the genre to see what their websites offer in comparison to Boheme.

Target Audience

To build the website with the client’s business in mind it was important to guide the team through the process of making user personas to help keep those who support the business in mind when making design decisions throughout the project. Two user persona’s were developed to help the designers guide their development.

Client Needs

Before laying down anything design wise it was important to meet with the client to get a scope of what their needs looked like. I asked them to fill out an incoming client form with a multiple of questions like what verbs they wanted the website to feel like in addition to other websites within their genre that they appreciated. After getting a feeling of what the client wanted I set up routine meetings that worked with both parties to ensure that throughout the process the client could help us tailor the product to their specified needs and offer feedback on our progress as a team.

Organizing

User Flow

Before starting to compose the wire frame composition the team set up a user flow of all the possibilities of interactions on the site to ensure that any page in the interaction is not missed and that everything is accounted for.

Wireframing

To assign deliverables for the pages to come for each designer I wanted a set of wire-frames that would help guide each individual on the project to help bring a cohesive layout to life. In addition to helping with cohesiveness the wire-frames helped break down individual sections of sub pages that could be assigned to each designer. After composing a set of wire-frames we got them approved by the client in terms of layout and direction for the project moving forward.

Design Guide

Before beginning the project I wanted to make a Design Guide to ensure all the sections of the page looked consistent. The design guide was shifted around after the initial creation of it as the group saw fitting, and every change and decision was approved upon by our client.

Developing

Insight into Developing Process

Phase One:
This project happened simultaneously, meaning that the design team would formulate 1-2 pages, we would receive feedback from the client, revise the pages and then hand them off to the tech team to develop. Generally projects do not happen in conjunction like this, but due to the time constraint of this project needing to be completed over the summer I found it fit to organize the project in this way. To consolidate progress we created a git-hub for people to pull their progress throughout the project.

Phase Two:
After formulating each page, I helped point out discrepancies between the prototype and the live code. A second round of deliverables were assigned for each page to ensure correct formatting.

Phase Three:
After each desktop view was formulated, the tech team utilized mobile queries to format the page for mobile. These first three phases were repeated for each part of the project until completion.

Phase Four:
Finally we chose GIT-Pages to host the website and changed the URL over to the new version of the web-page. This project was an ambitious challenge that could not of been completed without the amazing tech and design team at DCSC.

Reflection

In Conclusion...

This project taught me a lot of impactful skills that I can foresee myself using for the rest of my career. By essentially running my own design firm I got to learn a lot of lessons that would not have been taught to me by a traditional ux internship. I had the chance to build a client experience and learned the importance of precise organization and communication to make a good impression with those you work for. In addition to this, this was the first time I had to manage so many different people across different disciplines. By having to schedule and manage each individual contributor it shaped my understanding of leadership and equity within a project. This project especially taught me how important it is to work together. Through our comprehensive critique sessions I learned so much about how to utilize your team to make the best design decisions. I fully understand how collaboration is a winning strategy for better design. I look forward to applying the lessons learned throughout this project in my next career endeavor.
